DIY Repair Guides

Real fixes for real people. No fancy words, no shop rates. Just step-by-step help that gets you back on the road, back in your house, or back to work.

How to Change a Flat Tire

What You Need: Spare tire, jack, lug wrench, wheel chocks (or a brick/block), flashlight if it is dark
  1. Find a safe spot. Pull completely off the road. Turn on your hazard lights. Put the car in park and set the parking brake.
  2. Block the wheels. Put a brick, block, or wheel chock behind the tire diagonal from the flat. If the front left is flat, block the back right.
  3. Loosen the lug nuts. Use the lug wrench to turn each nut counter-clockwise about one full turn. Do NOT take them all the way off yet. They will be tight — use your foot on the wrench if you need to.
  4. Jack up the car. Find the jack point under your car (check your owner manual — it is usually a metal notch behind the front tire or in front of the back tire). Crank the jack until the flat tire is off the ground.
  5. Take off the lug nuts. Now unscrew them all the way and set them somewhere safe — do not lose them.
  6. Pull off the flat tire. Grab it with both hands and pull straight toward you. It might be stuck — wiggle it or kick the top lightly.
  7. Put on the spare. Line up the holes with the bolts and push it on. Put the lug nuts back on by hand first — tighten them in a star pattern (top, bottom, left, right) so the wheel goes on evenly.
  8. Lower the car. Crank the jack down until the tire touches the ground. Now use the wrench to tighten the lug nuts as tight as you can.
  9. Put everything away. Throw the flat in your trunk, put the jack and wrench back, and drive to a tire shop as soon as you can. Spares are not meant for long distances or fast speeds.
HardLife Tip: Check your spare tire pressure every few months. A flat spare is useless when you actually need it.

How to Change a Deadbolt Door Lock

What You Need: Phillips screwdriver, new deadbolt kit, tape measure
  1. Buy the right size. Measure the distance from the edge of your door to the center of the old lock hole. Most are 2-3/8 inches or 2-3/4 inches. The new box will say which one it fits.
  2. Remove the old lock. Open the door. On the inside, you will see two screws on the thumb turn plate. Unscrew them. The outside keypad or keyhole piece will fall out — hold it so it does not hit the floor.
  3. Take out the latch. On the edge of the door, unscrew the two screws holding the metal latch plate. Pull the latch out.
  4. Clean the hole. Blow out any dust or wood chips so the new lock slides in easy.
  5. Install the new latch. Slide it into the edge hole. The slanted side should face the direction the door closes. Screw the plate on.
  6. Put the outside piece on. Slide the long metal bar through the latch. Hold the outside keypad/keyhole against the door.
  7. Attach the inside piece. Line up the screw holes with the outside piece. Put in the two long screws and tighten them. Do not overtighten — you can strip the threads.
  8. Test it. Close the door and turn the thumb turn. The bolt should slide smoothly into the door frame hole. If it is too high or low, adjust the strike plate on the frame.
HardLife Tip: If the new bolt does not line up with the old hole in the frame, you can move the strike plate up or down. Just unscrew it, chisel a little if needed, and screw it back.

How to Clean and Adjust a 4-Wheeler Carburetor

What You Need: Phillips screwdriver, flathead screwdriver, carb cleaner spray, compressed air (or a straw and your mouth), small bowl, rags, gloves, safety glasses
  1. Turn off the gas. Find the fuel valve under the tank and turn it to OFF. If there is no valve, pinch the fuel line with a clamp.
  2. Take the carb off. Unscrew the clamp where the rubber boot meets the engine. Unscrew the clamp where the air box hose meets the carb. Pull the carb free. Some gas will drip — have a rag ready.
  3. Drain the bowl. On the bottom of the carb, there is a screw. Unscrew it and let the gas drain into your bowl. Look at the gas — if it is brown or has dirt, that was your problem.
  4. Take the bowl off. There are usually 4 screws on the bottom. Take them out and pull the bowl off. You will see a float (looks like a little plastic boat) and a jet (a small brass screw with a hole in it).
  5. Spray everything. Use carb cleaner to spray into every tiny hole you see. The jet, the needle, the passages — spray until the cleaner runs clear. Do NOT poke wire into the holes — you can ruin them.
  6. Blow it dry. Use compressed air or a straw to blow through every hole. If you cannot blow through it, it is still clogged — spray again.
  7. Put it back together. The float should move up and down freely. Put the bowl back on with the 4 screws. Do not overtighten — they strip easy.
  8. Reinstall on the 4-wheeler. Slide the carb back onto the boot, tighten both clamps, turn the gas back on.
  9. Start it up. It might take a few cranks because the bowl is empty. Once it runs, let it warm up for 2 minutes.
  10. Adjust the idle. Find the idle screw on the side — it is usually a big plastic knob or a small metal screw. Turn it clockwise to raise idle, counter-clockwise to lower it. You want it to idle smooth without dying or revving too high.
HardLife Tip: If your 4-wheeler sits for more than 2 weeks, turn the gas off and let it run until it dies. This empties the carb and stops gunk from building up.

How to Set Up Your Welder

What You Need: Welder, welding helmet, gloves, thick long-sleeve shirt, welding wire or rods, ground clamp, metal practice pieces
  1. Plug it in right. Check your welder plug. If it is a big 220V plug, you need a dryer outlet or welder outlet. If it is a regular 110V plug, any wall outlet works but do not run extension cords longer than 25 feet.
  2. Set up outside or in a garage with the door open. Welding makes toxic smoke. You need airflow. No welding in a closed basement.
  3. Put on your gear. Helmet down, gloves on, long sleeves buttoned, pants over your boots. One sunburn from welding flash and you will never skip this step again.
  4. Clamp the ground. The ground clamp must touch clean bare metal. Rust, paint, or grease will make your arc jump and sputter. Grind a spot if you have to.
  5. Load your wire or rod. For MIG: open the side panel, feed the wire through the liner, into the torch. Tighten the rollers so they grip but do not crush the wire. For stick: clamp the rod in the stinger.
  6. Set your heat. Look at the chart inside your welder lid or manual. Thinner metal = lower amps. Thicker metal = higher amps. When in doubt, start low and turn up if the weld looks cold and tall.
  7. Set your wire speed. MIG only. The rule is: the crackling should sound like frying bacon. If it pops and spits, wire speed is too high. If the wire melts back to the tip and sticks, speed is too low.
  8. Practice on scrap. Hold the torch at a 45-degree angle, drag it slowly toward you. Watch the puddle — it should be shiny and smooth, not bumpy or full of holes.
HardLife Tip: Your first welds will look like bird poop. That is normal. Everyone starts there. Keep practicing on scrap until you can lay a straight bead before touching your real project.

Welding for Dummies: The Basics

What You Need: Same as above — helmet, gloves, long sleeves, and a willingness to burn yourself a few times

What is welding? It is using electricity to melt metal together. Think of it as a hot glue gun for steel. The electricity creates an arc — a super hot spark — that melts the base metal and the filler metal (wire or rod) into one solid piece.

The 3 Main Types

  • MIG (Wire Welding): Easiest for beginners. You pull a trigger and wire feeds out automatically. Good for thin metal, auto body, and fences.
  • Stick (Arc Welding): Cheapest and most portable. You hold a rod and scratch it like a match to start the arc. Messier but works on thick metal and rusty stuff.
  • TIG: Precise and clean, but hard to learn. You hold a torch in one hand and feed filler rod with the other. Used for aluminum and pretty welds.

Safety Rules (Do Not Skip)

  • Wear a helmet with at least a #10 shade lens. #11 or #12 is better for stick.
  • Never weld without gloves. The UV light will give you a sunburn and the metal is 3000+ degrees.
  • Weld in a ventilated area. The smoke contains manganese and other metals that mess up your brain over time.
  • Keep a fire extinguisher nearby. Sparks fly 15-20 feet and will light up paper, gas cans, or dry grass.
  • Do not look at the arc without a helmet. Even a quick glance can damage your eyes.

Reading Your Weld

  • Good weld: Smooth, flat, straight lines that look like stacked dimes.
  • Too hot: Thin, burned holes, lots of spatter. Turn down the heat.
  • Too cold: Tall, bumpy, sitting on top of the metal like a worm. Turn up the heat or slow down.
  • Dirty metal: Porosity — little holes everywhere. Grind the rust off and try again.
HardLife Tip: Buy 10 pounds of scrap steel from a junkyard or metal yard. Practice running straight beads until you can do it with your eyes closed. Then practice making T-joints and butt joints. Do not touch your real project until you can lay a decent bead every time.
