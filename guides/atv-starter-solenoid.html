How to Test and Replace a Starter Solenoid on Any ATV (Step-by-Step)

📅 Updated April 2026 ⏱️ 30 minutes 💰 Saves $120-180 🛠️ Beginner friendly
Difficulty: Easy

Why This Guide Exists

Your ATV clicks but won't turn over. The starter spins fine when you jump it directly. The battery reads 12.6 volts. Nine times out of ten, the starter solenoid is the culprit. Shops charge $150-200 to swap a $15 part that takes 20 minutes. This guide shows you exactly how to test it with a multimeter and replace it yourself — no special tools, no mechanic needed.

🛠️ What You Need

  • Digital multimeter (or test light)
  • 10mm wrench or socket
  • Replacement solenoid ($12-25)
  • Dielectric grease (optional)
  • Phone/camera for reference photos

Safety first: Disconnect the negative battery terminal before working on any electrical component. ATVs have enough current to weld a wrench to the frame if you short positive to ground.

1

Locate Your Starter Solenoid

On most ATVs, the solenoid sits near the battery or along the frame rail between the battery and starter motor. It's a small cylinder (about 2 inches long) with two large posts and one or two small wires.

Common locations by brand:

• Yamaha Grizzly/Kodiak: Right side, near battery box
• Honda Rancher/Recon: Under front fender, left side
• Polaris Sportsman: Near radiator, right side
• Chinese quads (TaoTao, Coolster): Beside battery tray

[Photo: Solenoid location on Yamaha Grizzly 660]

Pro tip: Take a photo of the wiring before you disconnect anything. Color codes vary by year and model. A reference photo saves you from guessing later.

2

Test the Solenoid With a Multimeter

You don't need to remove it to test it. Set your multimeter to DC volts (20V range) and follow these checks:

Test 1 — Power in: Red probe on the battery-side large post. Black probe on ground (frame bolt). Turn key to "start." You should see 12+ volts. If not, your problem is upstream — battery, ignition switch, or safety switches.

Test 2 — Trigger wire: Red probe on the small wire terminal (usually spade connector). Black on ground. Turn key to start. You should see 12+ volts here too. No voltage means the solenoid isn't getting the signal to engage.

Test 3 — Power out: Red probe on the starter-side large post. Black on ground. Turn key to start. If you have power going IN (Test 1) and trigger (Test 2) but nothing coming OUT here, the solenoid is dead.

[Photo: Multimeter testing solenoid posts]
3

The Click Test (No Multimeter Needed)

If you don't have a multimeter, use a screwdriver to jump the two large posts. Touch the metal shaft across both posts at the same time. If the starter spins and the engine cranks, your solenoid is definitely bad.

Sparks will fly. This is normal — you're completing the circuit the solenoid should complete. Wear safety glasses and don't touch the frame with the screwdriver.

4

Remove the Old Solenoid

Disconnect the negative battery terminal first. Then:

1. Remove the two large cables (10mm nut). Battery cable first, then starter cable.
2. Disconnect the small trigger wire(s). These usually pull straight off.
3. Remove the mounting bolt (10mm). The solenoid will drop free.

Clean the cable ends with a wire brush if they're corroded. Green or white buildup means high resistance — even a new solenoid won't work right with dirty connections.

[Photo: Removing cables from old solenoid]
5

Install the New Solenoid

Mount the new solenoid in the same orientation. Reconnect in reverse order:

1. Mount bolt finger-tight
2. Small trigger wire(s)
3. Starter cable (large post)
4. Battery cable (large post)
5. Tighten everything to snug — don't overtighten
6. Reconnect negative battery terminal

Apply a thin coat of dielectric grease to the small wire connection if you have it. This prevents corrosion.

[Photo: New solenoid installed with cables connected]
6

Test Your Fix

Turn the key to "on." Hit the start button. The engine should crank immediately — no clicks, no delays. Let it run for 30 seconds, then shut it off and try again.

If it still clicks, check:
• Battery voltage under load (should stay above 9.6V while cranking)
• Ground connections (frame to engine block)
• Starter motor itself (may be drawing too much current)

Pro tip: If your ATV sits for months, the solenoid contacts can corrode from moisture. Start it weekly or install a battery disconnect switch to prevent drain.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use a car starter solenoid on my ATV?

No. ATV solenoids are rated for lower amperage and have different mounting. A car solenoid may work temporarily but will overheat and fail. Buy the correct part for your model — they're $12-25.

Why does my solenoid keep burning out?

Three common causes: (1) Low battery voltage makes the solenoid chatter and overheat, (2) Starter motor is failing and drawing too much current, (3) Poor ground connection causes arcing at the contacts.

My ATV clicks once then nothing. Is it the solenoid?

One loud click usually means the solenoid is engaging but the starter isn't spinning. Test by jumping the large posts with a screwdriver. If the starter spins, replace the solenoid. If it doesn't, your starter motor is bad.

How long does a starter solenoid last?

5-10 years under normal use. They fail faster if the ATV sits unused (corrosion) or if the battery is weak (chattering). A $15 solenoid is cheap insurance — replace it proactively if yours is over 8 years old.
