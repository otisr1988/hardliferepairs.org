Dryer Not Heating? How to Fix It in 45 Minutes (Step-by-Step)

📅 Updated April 2026 ⏱️ 45 minutes 💰 Saves $200-250 🛠️ Beginner friendly
Difficulty: Easy

Why This Guide Exists

Your dryer tumbles but clothes come out damp and cold. The repair shop wants $200 plus parts to fix it. In reality, 80% of "no heat" dryer problems come from three cheap parts: the heating element, thermal fuse, or high-limit thermostat. All three cost under $30 and swap out with basic hand tools. This guide walks you through diagnosing which part failed and replacing it — no experience needed.

🛠️ What You Need

  • Phillips and flathead screwdrivers
  • 1/4-inch nut driver or socket
  • Multimeter (for testing)
  • Replacement part ($15-30)
  • Vacuum with hose attachment
  • Phone for reference photos

Unplug the dryer before doing anything. Dryers run on 240 volts — enough to kill you. Pull the plug from the wall. If it's hardwired, flip the breaker and verify with a non-contact voltage tester.

1

Figure Out Which Part Failed

Before you buy parts, you need to know what's broken. There are three main culprits for a dryer that runs but doesn't heat:

Heating element: The coil that glows red and generates heat. Costs $20-40. Most common failure.

Thermal fuse: A safety switch that blows if the dryer overheats. Costs $8-15. Usually caused by clogged venting.

High-limit thermostat: Cycles the element on and off. Costs $12-20. Fails from age or lint buildup.

Quick test without tools: Start the dryer on high heat. Wait 5 minutes, then open the door. If the drum is slightly warm but not hot, the element is probably broken (partial failure). If completely cold, check the thermal fuse first.

[Photo: Dryer back panel removed showing heating element location]
2

Access the Heating Components

Most dryers open from the back or front. Here's how for the top brands:

Whirlpool/Kenmore/Maytag (most common):
1. Unplug dryer and pull away from wall
2. Remove back panel (8-12 screws, 1/4-inch)
3. Heating element is a large metal box on the right side
4. Thermal fuse sits on the blower housing (left side, white plastic with two wires)

Samsung/LG:
1. Remove top panel (screws in back)
2. Remove control panel
3. Remove front panel and door assembly
4. Element is at the bottom rear

GE/Hotpoint:
1. Remove front bottom panel (two clips at top corners)
2. Element is at the bottom center

Pro tip: Take a photo before you disconnect any wires. Dryers use different wire colors across brands and years. A photo is your insurance policy.

3

Test the Thermal Fuse (Do This First)

The thermal fuse is the cheapest fix and the most common cause. It's a white plastic piece, about the size of a quarter, with two wires.

With a multimeter: Set to continuity or ohms. Touch one probe to each terminal. A good fuse beeps (continuity) or reads near zero ohms. A blown fuse reads "OL" or infinity — no connection.

Without a multimeter: Remove the fuse and shake it. If you hear a rattle inside, it's blown. (Not 100% reliable, but works in a pinch.)

Critical: If the fuse is blown, your vent is clogged. Clean the vent hose and outside cap before installing the new fuse, or you'll blow the next one in a week.

[Photo: Testing thermal fuse with multimeter showing continuity]
4

Test the Heating Element

The heating element looks like a coiled spring inside a metal housing. Set your multimeter to ohms (Ω).

Test across the element terminals: You should read 5-15 ohms depending on your dryer model. Check the label on your dryer or Google "[your model] heating element resistance."

Test element to ground: One probe on the element terminal, one on the metal frame. You should read infinity ("OL"). Any reading here means the element is shorted to ground — replace immediately.

Visual check: Look for breaks in the coil. A broken coil = dead element. Sometimes the break is obvious; sometimes you need to look close with a flashlight.

[Photo: Heating element with visible coil break]
5

Test the Thermostats

Dryers have 2-4 thermostats (cycling thermostat, high-limit thermostat, thermal cutoff). They're round metal discs with two wires.

Test each one: Set multimeter to continuity. Touch probes to both terminals. All good thermostats show continuity (beep). If any read "OL," replace that thermostat.

Don't guess which is which. They're usually labeled or color-coded. Take a photo before removing. Order the exact replacement by part number — not all thermostats are interchangeable.

[Photo: Thermostats on blower housing with labels visible]
6

Replace the Failed Part

Once you know what's broken, swap it:

Heating element:
1. Disconnect wires (photo first)
2. Remove mounting screws (usually 2-4)
3. Pull element straight out
4. Install new element, reconnect wires
5. Don't overtighten — strip the screws and you're done

Thermal fuse:
1. Disconnect two wires
2. Remove mounting screw
3. Install new fuse exactly where the old one sat
4. Reconnect wires (polarity doesn't matter)

Thermostat:
1. Disconnect wires
2. Remove mounting screws
3. Install new thermostat in same position
4. Reconnect wires

[Photo: Installing new heating element in housing]
7

Clean Everything While You're In There

This is where you prevent the next breakdown. Lint buildup kills dryers.

1. Vacuum the entire interior — element housing, blower wheel, motor area
2. Pull the lint trap and wash it with soap and water (detergent residue blocks airflow)
3. Disconnect the vent hose from the back and vacuum it out
4. Go outside and check the vent cap — remove any bird nests or lint clogs
5. Reconnect the vent hose with a new clamp if the old one is loose

Pro tip: If your vent hose is the white vinyl accordion style, replace it with rigid metal ducting. The vinyl traps lint, creates fire hazards, and is illegal in most building codes now. Rigid duct costs $15 and lasts forever.

8

Reassemble and Test

Put the panels back on. Plug in the dryer. Run it empty on high heat for 10 minutes.

Check: Open the door after 5 minutes — should be hot. After 10 minutes, the dryer should feel warm on the outside but not burning hot.

If it still doesn't heat:
• Check that you plugged all wires back in
• Verify the breaker didn't trip (240V dryers need both legs)
• Test the timer — some dryers won't heat if the timer is broken
• Check the power cord — loose connections cause partial voltage

Frequently Asked Questions

Why does my thermal fuse keep blowing?

Clogged venting is the #1 cause. Check the outside vent cap — lint, bird nests, and snow block airflow. Also check the blower wheel for lint buildup. A restricted vent makes the dryer overheat and blow the fuse every time.

Can I run my dryer without a thermal fuse?

Never. The thermal fuse prevents house fires. Running without it is like removing the brakes from your car. If you're stuck waiting for a part, air-dry your clothes. Don't bypass safety devices.

My dryer heats sometimes but not always. What's wrong?

Intermittent heat usually means the cycling thermostat is failing. It works when cold but sticks open when hot. The high-limit thermostat can do the same. Test both with a multimeter while heating — they should open and close at set temperatures.

How long should a dryer heating element last?

8-15 years with clean vents. Clogged vents kill elements in 3-5 years from overheating. Clean your vent every 6 months and the element will outlast the drum bearings.

Is it worth fixing a 15-year-old dryer?

If the repair is under $50 (thermal fuse or element), yes. If you need a motor, belt, or drum rollers, compare to a new $400-600 dryer. A $20 fix on an old dryer buys you 2-3 more years. A $200 repair doesn't make sense.
